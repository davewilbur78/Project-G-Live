Subject
Jacob SINGER  /  Yankel SPRINGER
b. 3 December 1895, Lechowitz, Volhynia, Russian Empire  ·  d. 2 February 1981, Oakland, Bergen Co., NJ  ·  GEDCOM @I327@
Why This Question Requires Proof

The subject appears in all U.S. records as Jacob Singer or variants (Jack, Jake). He appears in the 1907 passenger manifest as Yankel Springer, under his family's original surname. No single document explicitly bridges the two names. The GEDCOM asserts the connection but does not prove it. This case study constructs that proof.

A preponderance of evidence supports the conclusion that Yankel Springer of Lechowitz, Volhynia is the same person as Jacob Singer of the Bronx, New York. In November 1907, a child named Yankel Springer, approximately eleven years old, arrived at the Port of New York aboard the S.S. Marion, having sailed from Antwerp, Belgium. He traveled with his mother Chine Springer and two younger siblings. The contact named for the family was Schmuel Springer of New York City, identified as the father. Yankel and his siblings were recorded as born in Lechowce, a transliteration of the same Volhynian town referenced throughout the Singer family's American records as Lechowitz. Twelve years later, in April 1919, a man named Jacob Singer appeared before the Supreme Court of the State of New York to petition for naturalization. He stated that he had been born on 3 December 1895 in Volhynia, Russia; that he had emigrated through Antwerp, Belgium; and that he had arrived at the Port of New York on November 28, 1907, aboard the vessel Marion. The U.S. Department of Labor's Certificate of Arrival, attached to the petition, certified the arrival of Singer, Yankel, on that date and vessel. The birth year, port of departure, vessel, and arrival date stated in the petition correspond precisely to the passenger manifest entry for Yankel Springer. The father named on the 1907 manifest, Schmuel Springer, is consistent with the GEDCOM identification of Jacob Singer's father as Samuel Singer, whose documented birth surname was Springer and whose own father was Tzvi Dov Springer, also recorded as Berl Springer. The surname transition from Springer to Singer was a family-level change that occurred between 1907 and 1915, the period between Yankel's arrival and his first appearance in American records under the Singer name. This pattern of Ashkenazi immigrant families Anglicizing surnames in the years immediately following immigration is well documented in early twentieth-century New York. Two apparent conflicts emerge from the record. The 1907 manifest records the family name as Springer; the 1919 Certificate of Arrival records the name as Singer, Yankel. This reflects the timing of each record rather than a contradiction: the manifest was made at the moment of arrival under the original surname, while the immigration service in 1919 cross-referenced the arrival against the name then in use, as was standard practice. The second conflict, between the mother's name on the manifest (Chine Springer) and the mother's name in the GEDCOM (Minnie Jacobs), remains unresolved and constitutes a separate research question that does not affect the identity chain between Yankel Springer and Jacob Singer. On the basis of this evidence, which is reasonably exhaustive within the available American record, the identity of Yankel Springer and Jacob Singer is established to the level of proof required by the Genealogical Proof Standard. The convergence of vessel, date, port, place of origin, birth year, and father's name across independent records leaves no reasonable alternative candidate. The remaining gap -- the absence of a document explicitly bridging the two surnames -- is acknowledged, addressed through the preponderance chain, and noted as a target for future research.
